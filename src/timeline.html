Intel's History and Innovation-The Timeline

1968-1979

1968
  • Intel Formed by Bob Noyce and Gordon Moore, previous employees of Fairchild Semiconductor.
  • Andy Grove soon joined and became known as a “three-headed monster” by Bob Noyce.
1969
  • Japanese calculator manufacturer Busicom asked Intel to design a chipset for programmable calculators.
  • Intel engineer Ted Hoff created a four-chip set with a general purpose that the device would be able to access the instructions from memory.
  • Federico Faggin teamed with Hoff to develop the 4004, the first microprocessor, that had as much computing power as the first electronic computer, the ENIAC.
1971
  • 4004 is released
  • Intel opened offices in Tokyo, France and the U.K. with Germany a year later
  • Gordon was approached with idea that would later become the PC
    • The idea was to put the 8080 microprocessor with a keyboard and monitor then sell it onto the home market.
    • Gordon’s response, “What’s it good for? And the only answer was that a house-wife could keep her recipes on it. I personally didn’t see anything useful in it, so we never gave it another thought.”
1975
  • After 8080 Processor entered the market, the Intel Advanced Processor Architecture 432 (iAPX) was born.
  • Purpose was to create a new microprocessor architecture, a new system architecture and new operating system software as well.
  • It was to feature fault tolerance and self-checking hardware, which was later added into the Pentium processor.
  • Proved to be too complex and was phased out in the 1980’s.
1979
  • Although Intel’s 16-bit 8086 and 8-bit 8088 received steady purchases, Intel also received completion from Motorola’s 68000 designs.
  • In response, Intel launched campaign in 1980 to make the 8086/8088 architecture to become the industry standard under the code-name “Operation Crush” with the objective to generate 2,000 design wins within the year.
  • Operation crush a success with 2,500 design wins.
  • IBM chose 8088 as the microprocessor for its first PC. First time IBM chose an outside vendor for a microprocessor.

1985-2000

1985
  • The Intel386 chip is developed which was supported from 1985 to 2007.
  • Previously second sourced manufacturing of chips and selling Intel products to other companies in exchange for royalties and/or rights to other products.
  • When Intel’s microprocessor line increased in value and candidates did not keep up with the pace of exchange, Intel decided to be the sole manufacturer.
  • Andy notes during the time that “I think AMD, especially thought we were bluffing. They did not meet their commitments, thinking we’d need their help eventually to meet demand”.
1989
  • Intel started a “Red X” campaign which consisted of a red x being put over 286 to put a message to switch over to the Intel386 SX and 32-bit machines.
  • First time sending a message to PC users with the new Windows operating system coming soon.
  • Found to be very successful, Intel was afraid that if the campaign failed it would have killed the 286 and no one moving to the new chip.
1993
  • Intel introduces Pentium processor, five times more powerful than the i486 processor from 1989.
  • After two years, the Intel Inside campaign helps shoot Intel to become the third most valuable brand in the world.
  • Fortune cover has Intel Pentium processor as the leader in “The New Computer Revolution”.
1994
  • Jeopardy contestant correctly answers that Intel chips power about 85% of all desktop computers.
1995
  • Space shuttle Endeavor conduct first real-time, PC-based conference from space using Intel Proshare videoconferencing technology.
1996
  • Ted Hoff, Stan Mazor and Federico Faggin inducted into National Inventors Hall of Fame for developing microprocessor 25 years before.
1998
  • Intel announces first high-performance, low-power processors based on StrongARM technology for handheld computing and communication devices.
  • Intel launches first processor for PC market, Intel Celeron processor.
  • Intel Pentium II Xeon processor for mid-range and high-end workstation and server markets.

2000-2012

2000
  • Launched the Intel XScale microarchitecture and Intel PRO/Wireless LAN PC cards.
2001
  • Intel develop extreme ultraviolet lithography, considered key to making smaller semiconductor devices in the future.
  • Itanium and Xeon processors support high performance workstations and servers.
2002
  • Hyper-Threading Technology improves multitasking by enabling multiple threads of information to run simultaneously on one processor.
2003
  • Intel introduces PXA800F cellular processor, microchip that combines cell phones and handheld computers.
  • Centrino processor brings high performance, great battery life and integrated wireless LAN capability to thinner, lighter laptops.
2006
  • World’s first quad-core processor for servers and desktop.
2007
  • Intel Core 2 Quad offered for mainstream desktop computers.
  • New 45nm transistors breakthrough. 2 million Intel 45nm transistors can fit inside a period.
2008
  • Atom processors energy-efficient and low-cost designed to provide wireless capability for small mobile computing devices such as netbooks.
2012
  • Intel Xeon Phi Coprocessor after years of research marks a new era in high-performance computing.
